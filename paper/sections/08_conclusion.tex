\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented GAME-Mal, a malware family classifier that combines
reflection-aware dynamic-trace parsing with a sigmoid-gated
multi-head attention transformer. The gated architecture --- G1
position of Qiu et al.\ --- reaches macro F1 of $0.886$ on the
eight-family UMD subset, statistically indistinguishable from a
strong Random Forest baseline ($0.893$) and $17.7$ percentage
points above a direct reproduction of the Markov associative-rule
classifier of D'Angelo et al.\ ($0.709$), while exposing an
intrinsic, forward-pass-native explanation of its decisions
through its gate activations. Fusob, the minority ransomware
family, is a useful case in point: despite contributing only
$1.8\%$ of the corpus, it achieves perfect recall under GAME-Mal
whereas the statistical baseline collapses on it for want of
rule-mining evidence.

The broader contribution is \emph{methodological rather than
performance-based}. Our ablation against a plain (non-gated)
transformer of identical capacity shows the gate does not improve
raw accuracy at this corpus scale --- the plain baseline is
marginally better on every metric (F1 $0.897$ vs.\ $0.886$).
What the gate \emph{does} give is an always-on ranking signal
over APIs available in a single forward pass, with no
back-propagation, no reference sample, and no additional
compute. We view this as the relevant trade: a fraction of a
percentage point of accuracy in exchange for inference-time
explainability. On security-critical applications where model
behaviour must be explainable to a human analyst, this trade is
often the one worth making --- and reporting it honestly,
including the cases where the mechanism does not add raw
accuracy, is what lets future work separate interpretability
claims from performance claims.
