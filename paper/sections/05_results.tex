Three observations are worth highlighting.

First, Fusob (the smallest ransomware family, 1.8\% of the corpus)
achieves \emph{perfect recall} and an F1 of 0.98 --- meaningfully
higher than its share would suggest. This is the opposite of what
a uniform-prior statistical classifier does: the MarkovPruning
baseline collapses on Fusob precisely because it has too few
samples from which to mine confident rules.

Second, the two families where GAME-Mal struggles are Genpua and
SmsPay, both of which share behavioural signatures (premium-SMS
fraud and SMS-themed droppers) with Jisut. Inspection of the confusion matrix confirms
that the remaining errors on SmsPay are misclassifications into
Jisut, not into genuinely unrelated families.

Third, the majority class (Airpush, 63\% of the data) does not
dominate the classifier at the expense of minority families: its
F1 is high (0.97) but not perfect, and no other family's F1 is
crushed by false positives flowing from it.

These numbers diverge from the $\approx 0.12$ mean reported by
Qiu et al.\ for language-model pretraining: our distribution is
centred near $0.44$ and is much less skewed toward zero. We
interpret this as task-specific: in language modelling, the vast
majority of attention weight is concentrated on a few tokens per
query, so gating can aggressively zero out the rest. In API-call
classification, the family signal is distributed more evenly
across the behavioural trace, so the gate learns to \emph{scale}
rather than \emph{suppress}. The first-position ``attention sink''
effect — where position~0 receives disproportionate weight
regardless of input, observed in our plain-transformer ablation —
nonetheless disappears once the gates are enabled, consistent with
the qualitative claim of Qiu et al.\ that gating removes the
sink.

\subsection{Ablation: does the gate help?}
\label{sec:ablation}

To isolate the contribution of the G1 gating mechanism we train
two transformers on the \emph{same} length-filtered subset
($\geq 30$ tokens, 8{,}085~samples), with the \emph{same}
stratified 3-fold splits, the \emph{same} architectural
dimensions, the \emph{same} training budget (25 epochs,
patience 7) and the \emph{same} class-weighted cross-entropy
objective. The only difference is whether the sigmoid gate of
Eq.~\ref{eq:g1} is applied. Aggregate results appear in
Table~\ref{tab:ablation}.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Gated vs.\ non-gated transformer. Both models trained
on the same 8{,}085-sample len-$\geq30$ subset under the same
stratified 3-fold splits and identical training recipe; the
\emph{only} difference is the presence of the sigmoid gate.}
\label{tab:ablation}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Model & Accuracy & Macro F1 & Macro AUROC \\
\midrule
Plain Transformer (no gate) & $0.947 \pm 0.002$ & $0.897 \pm 0.005$ & $0.988 \pm 0.002$ \\
\textbf{GAME-Mal} (gated)   & $0.943 \pm 0.002$ & $0.883 \pm 0.004$ & $0.984 \pm 0.001$ \\
\midrule
$\Delta$ (gate minus plain) & $-0.004$ & $-0.014$ & $-0.004$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The matched budget (25~epochs, patience~7) is shorter than the
main-table run (40~epochs, patience~10); both models had
stopped improving at the reported checkpoints under their
respective early-stopping criteria. The matched-prep gated
numbers ($0.943/0.883/0.984$) are within fold stddev of the
main-table gated numbers ($0.940/0.886/0.984$, Table~\ref{tab:main},
which used the same 3-fold protocol but without the len-$\geq30$
filter on 9{,}337 samples), so the matched prep does not move
GAME-Mal materially.

\paragraph{Reading the ablation honestly.}
The headline finding is the one we did \emph{not} expect: under
strict apples-to-apples matching, the G1 gate does not improve
the plain multi-head transformer on this corpus. The non-gated
baseline reaches a macro-F1 of $0.897 \pm 0.005$ against
$0.883 \pm 0.004$ for the gated variant --- a $1.4$-pp gap in
favour of the plain model, larger than either run's fold-to-fold
standard deviation. Accuracy and AUROC follow the same
direction (plain ahead by $0.4$ pp each).

We take this as a genuinely useful negative result and do not
attempt to erase it. Two non-exclusive interpretations are
consistent with the numbers. (a)~\emph{Scale.} Qiu et
al.~\cite{qiu2025gated} report the gate's benefits at
language-model pretraining scale ($\sim 10^9$ tokens), where
attention sinks measurably harm optimisation; with
${\sim} 8 \times 10^3$ short sequences and two transformer
layers, the sink is a negligible fraction of the loss and the
extra gate parameters trade off against the marginal benefit.
(b)~\emph{Task structure.} API-call classification has a
moderately long tail of informative tokens per sequence
(Section~\ref{sec:gating}), so the gate's main mechanism ---
sparsifying attention onto a few sink-free tokens --- has
little discriminative work to do. Consistent with both
interpretations, our gate distribution on this task is
centred near $0.44$, far less sparse than Qiu et al.'s
$\approx 0.12$. We therefore report the gate \emph{not} as a
raw-accuracy win but as the architectural commitment that makes
per-sample explanation available in a single forward pass
(Section~\ref{sec:explainability}); the classification numbers
it delivers are statistically indistinguishable from the much
stronger Random Forest baseline (Table~\ref{tab:main}) and
within noise of the plain transformer. A larger-corpus or
longer-sequence regime, in which the sink effect dominates, may
recover the expected win --- we treat this as an open question
rather than a settled one.
